#makinglawhuman · DPDP Field Research

I stress-tested Indian companies
before the Rules were published.

Weeks of direct conversations with founders, security leads, and vendors — before the DPDP Rules were finalised. What I found was not non-compliance as exception. It was non-compliance as default.

0/5
Companies could meet the 72-hour breach response window
18+
Sites with broken consent flows documented
15+
Published pieces in the #makinglawhuman series
01
Breach Response
Five companies. None could detect, verify, escalate, and notify within 72 hours. One security lead: "forget reporting, we'd be lucky to confirm it." Another's breach plan was a Slack channel. The 72-hour window is the first thing enforcement will test — and almost no Indian company is ready for it.
02
Consent Flows
Pre-checked boxes on 8 sites. Bundled consent on 10. One app demanded cookie acceptance before showing its homepage. None passed Section 6's standard: free, specific, informed, unconditional, unambiguous. Consent was treated as a UX checkbox — not a legal obligation with enforcement consequence.
03
Vendor Transparency
15 vendors contacted. Answers were vague, outdated, or absent. Three admitted using sub-processors they "cannot fully disclose." Processor accountability — a DPDP core obligation — was universally treated as someone else's problem.
04
SDF Awareness
A health-tech startup and an ed-tech platform processing children's data had never heard "Significant Data Fiduciary." Neither knew about DPIAs, independent audit requirements, or DPO obligations. The obligations most likely to generate enforcement action were the least known.
"DPDP will not fail because the law is too strict.
It will fail because too many companies will start preparing
only after they've already been caught."
