01
Breach Response
Spoke to five companies. None could detect, verify, escalate, and notify within 72 hours. One security lead: "forget reporting, we'd be lucky to confirm it." Another's breach plan was a Slack channel. The 72-hour window is not theoretical — it is the first thing enforcement will test.
02
Consent Flows
Pre-checked boxes on 8 sites. Bundled consent on 10. One app demanded cookie acceptance before showing the homepage. None passed DPDP's Section 6 standard: free, specific, informed, unconditional, unambiguous. Consent was universally treated as a UX checkbox, not a legal obligation.
03
Vendor Transparency
15 vendors contacted. Answers were vague, outdated, or absent. Three admitted using sub-processors they "cannot fully disclose." One founder: "our vendor has good reviews, we trust them." Processor accountability — a DPDP core obligation — was treated as someone else's problem.
04
SDF Awareness
A health-tech startup and an ed-tech platform processing children's data had never heard the term "Significant Data Fiduciary." Neither knew about DPIAs, independent audit requirements, or the obligation to appoint a DPO. The obligations most likely to generate enforcement action were the ones least known.

"DPDP will not fail because the law is too strict. It will fail because too many companies will start preparing only after they've already been caught."

— LinkedIn, post-Rules publication follow-up
